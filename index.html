NYCLED




This map represents the cross of data sets of address points (XY-data) and building footprints (shape-data), over time- in effect, a map of the structural growth of Manhattan. A building is a permanent marker of humanity. Once built, that space is rarely unoccupied again; if the building falls, another will quickly take its place and repopulate itself.

Each change of LED represents a historical moment in the story of this city: at the southern tip of the island the LEDs start where Giovanni de Verrazano first found NYC; an area to the north is settled by the Dutch, what they called "Haarlem"; the rapid climb up the island follows the expansions of the IRT, NYC's first public transportation system.

While maps are traditionally meant to be easy to read and consume, this map has a stronger consideration for the impression it sets. The LEDs start small in number and brightness, and then the map quickly reflects the speed and strength of New York City's development. Soon the entire island emanates light so bright- reflective of the structural growth of the city as well as its incredible energy- that any person near it cannot help but gawk at its spectacle.

This project was created with NYC Open Data, Processing, Arduino and Adafruit NeoPixels.


WhereRUV | tileset[0,1 - 4,1]

Hollywood is always using NYC as its center for the Apocalypse. It's not exactly unwarranted: NYC is an extremely influential city (and it's easy to quarantine). This map cannot be found in our time, or rather, our universe- it exists in post nuclear-accident in NY. Electromagnetic Pulses (EMPs) are intense bursts of electromagnetic energy created after a nuclear explosion, which has been hypothesized to have devastating effects on the power grid. In a retrograded society, the use of physical maps reemerges.

With many having fled underground into the subway, this map depicts all of the subway entrances, as well as radii plotted a 5, 10, and 15 minute-walks away. The choice of material is a fluorescent acrylic. Fluorescent acrylic has the ability to absorb UV light and slow it down into the visible light spectrum, an important ability given that the winter following a nuclear explosion increases UV levels an additional 300%, allowing the map to glow in otherwise dark situations.

The map is not initially identifiable as a map of New York City: the iconic oblong island of Manhattan is abstracted by the less identifiable Brooklyn and Queens. This abstraction is a result of zooming in on the land mass and the inclusion of other boroughs. The "zooming in" allows for a realistic depiction of the distance one could travel by foot in the post-apocalyptic New York City, but also speaks to the current convoluted relationship between accessibility and transportation in New York. Those who rely on public transportation the most are often the most alienated from the system, as its center is always the more economically prosperous parts of the city.

This project was made with laser cut fluorescent acrylic.

The Risogrpah is a faster and cheaper alternative to screen printing. Like screen printing, the “riso” prints a single color at time. A grayscale PDF is sent to the printer with 100% black representing the highest ink density and 0% representing the lowest ink density. The file is then burned onto a piece of banana paper, the master, which is applied around a drum of ink. The riso then pulls a single sheet of paper and rolls the drum over the sheet as ink is forced through the master. The Parsons Graphics Lab on the 10th floor of 2 West 13th Street operates an MZ790U, a dual color riso that is able to apply two colors in a single pass.

This series of booklets containing shades is the representation of the variety of producible hues with the current available spot colors: Flat Gold, Green, Midnight Blue,Smoky Teal, Fluorescent Pink, and Bright Red. (for the sake of conserving space, the booklet uses the following references for spot colors respectively: yellow, green, blue, teal, pink, red). In addition to hues, these booklets also display the range of moire patterns by taking into consideration the different halftone(43 Lines Per Inch, 71 lpi, 106 lpi, and a grain mode) modes.In other words, each page shows the values able to produced when a single color and halftone mode is overlapped with another color and halftone mode. Thus this is a Tetravigesimal Ordered-Pair color space. (I consider each of the color-halftone combinations like blue 43 and red 71 their own spot color).

In 1692 a Dutch author A. Boogart created the manuscript Klaer Lightende Spiegel der Verfkonst. The manuscript consisted of over 800 pages of different shades of color. Along with each shade was recipes and techniques of how to produce that color. This was the centuries old precursor to Pantone and their Pantone color matching system.

There’s no arguing that the desktop printer has been ubiquitous. And our familiarity with color space has been too and with it the CMYK color space has become printing vernacular. CMYK process uses four inks: Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Black(fyi: K is for Key). It is a subtractive system, that is, with a white background, inks reduces the reflected light. No ink = White. Max ink = Black.

In printing, the individual inks used to mix into different tones are called spot colors. In the desktop printer the spot colors are cyan, magenta, yellow and black.
